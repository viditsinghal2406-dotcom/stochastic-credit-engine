What is Credit Default Risk?

      
When a bank lends money or gives a credit card, there's always a chance the borrower might not pay it back. That risk — of a borrower defaulting (failing to repay) — is called Credit Default Risk.

      
Think of it like this: if you lend ₹100 to 100 friends, maybe 5 won't pay you back. The bank needs to know which 5, and when they'll stop paying.

      
Traditional approaches just label borrowers as "will default" or "won't default." STPA is more sophisticated — it models the entire financial trajectory of a borrower over time, incorporating uncertainty at every step.

      
Banks care about this because:

      
    
        
  • They need to hold capital reserves proportional to risk
    • 
        
  • They price credit cards and loans based on expected default losses
    • 
        
  • Regulatory bodies (RBI, Basel III) require risk measurement
    • 
        
  • Portfolio management requires knowing which borrowers to monitor
    • 
      

    

  


  

    

      
The Markov Chain Model

      
A Markov Chain is a mathematical system that moves between different "states" over time. The key property: where you go next depends only on where you are now — not where you've been.

      

        
EXCELLENT

        
→

        
GOOD

        
→

        
FAIR

        
→

        
STRESSED

        
→

        
DELINQUENT

        
→

        
DEFAULT

      

      
For credit risk, states represent credit health zones. A borrower in "FAIR" state might move to "GOOD" (improving) or "STRESSED" (worsening) next month. These probabilities form a Transition Matrix.

      
P = | P(EXCELLENT→EXCELLENT)  P(EXCELLENT→GOOD)  ... |
    | P(GOOD→EXCELLENT)       P(GOOD→GOOD)       ... |
    | ...                                            |
    
Each row sums to 1.0 (probabilities of all transitions from a state)

      
NovaCred's engine uses a Non-Homogeneous Markov Chain — the transition matrix shifts under different macro-economic conditions (recession, rate hikes, etc.)

    

  


  

    

      
The Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Diffusion Process

      
Your financial health score doesn't jump randomly — it drifts slowly and tends to revert to your long-run average. This is exactly what the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) process captures.

      
🎱 Imagine a ball on a hill with a spring attached to the middle. Random wind blows it around, but the spring always pulls it back toward the centre. That spring is mean-reversion.

      
dX_t = θ(μ - X_t)dt + σdW_t

Where:
  X_t  = Financial health score at time t
  θ    = Mean-reversion speed (spring strength)
  μ    = Long-run mean (where the spring pulls to)
  σ    = Volatility (how strong the wind is)
  dW_t = Random Brownian motion increment

      
Why OU over simpler random walk (GBM)?

      
    
        
  • Credit scores mean-revert — someone with a 750 score rarely drifts permanently to 500
    • 
        
  • GBM assumes unbounded drift — unrealistic for bounded scores (0–100)
    • 
        
  • OU has a known closed-form distribution — mathematically elegant
    • 
      

    

  


  

    

      
Jump Process — Modeling Life's Shocks

      
Life doesn't just drift slowly — sometimes it jumps. Job loss, medical emergency, divorce, unexpected tax bill. These sudden events cause discrete drops in financial health that the smooth OU process can't capture.

      
J(t) = Σ Y_i  for i = 1..N(t)

Where:
  N(t)  = Poisson process — number of shocks by time t
  λ     = Shock arrival rate (e.g. 1.5 shocks/year)
  Y_i   = Shock magnitude (random, from historical data)

Combined: dX_t = θ(μ-X_t)dt + σdW_t + dJ_t

      
Shock types in STPA:

      
    
        
  • Minor (60% of shocks): −3 to −8 pts — unexpected bill, small income dip
    • 
        
  • Moderate (30%): −10 to −20 pts — job change, medical expense
    • 
        
  • Severe (10%): −25 to −40 pts — layoff, bankruptcy trigger
    • 
        
  • Positive: +5 to +15 pts — promotion, debt payoff, windfall
    •

Monte Carlo Simulation

      
Instead of solving the math equations directly (which is hard for our combined model), STPA simulates 10,000 different possible futures for a borrower and counts outcomes.

      
🎲 Imagine rolling a dice 10,000 times to estimate the probability of rolling a 6. You could calculate it (1/6 ≈ 16.7%), or you could just roll and count. For complex financial systems, simulation is often the only option.

      
How it works:

      
    
        
  • Start with borrower's current financial health score
    • 
        
  • Apply OU drift for one month (tiny random step)
    • 
        
  • Randomly decide if a shock occurs this month (Poisson draw)
    • 
        
  • If shock → apply random magnitude (jump)
    • 
        
  • Record whether score crossed the default threshold
    • 
        
  • Repeat for 24 months → one path complete
    • 
        
  • Run 10,000 such paths
    • 
        
  • PD = fraction of paths that hit default
    • 
      

      
PD = (1/N) × Σ 1[min_t X_t^(n) < X_threshold]

Where N = 10,000 simulations
      X_threshold = 20 (our default boundary)

    

  


  

    

      
How Credit Scores Work

      
Credit scores (like CIBIL in India, FICO in the US) summarize your creditworthiness as a single number. NovaCred estimates your score using the same components as major bureaus:

      
    
        
  • Payment History (35%) — Do you pay on time? This matters most.
    • 
        
  • Credit Utilization (30%) — How much of your credit limit are you using? Keep below 30%.
    • 
        
  • Credit History Length (15%) — Older accounts help.
    • 
        
  • Credit Mix (10%) — Having both cards and loans is better than just one type.
    • 
        
  • New Applications (10%) — Too many recent applications hurt your score.
    • 
      

      
💡 The single best thing you can do for your credit score: never miss a payment. Set up auto-pay for at least the minimum amount.

      
Score ranges (CIBIL standard):

      
    
        
  • 🟢 750–900: Excellent — best rates, easiest approval
    • 
        
  • 🟡 700–749: Good — most products available
    • 
        
  • 🟠 650–699: Fair — limited products, higher rates
    • 
        
  • 🔴 600–649: Poor — very limited options
    • 
        
  • ⚫ Below 600: Very Poor — likely rejection
    •
